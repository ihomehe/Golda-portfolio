Internal announcement headline variations · Three options for the same update

Option A — Direct: Flexible work guidelines updated for 2026. Here's what changes and what stays the same.

Option B — Why-first: You asked for more flexibility. This is what we heard, and what we're doing about it.

Option C — Action-oriented: New flexible work policy takes effect May 1. No action needed now — but here's what to expect.

Each headline is defensible depending on the audience relationship and what the organization wants to signal. Option A is neutral and efficient. Option B builds trust by acknowledging the survey origin. Option C prioritizes clarity for anxious readers.

Voice note: strategic rationale
Short-form writing is where discipline shows. The LinkedIn post earns its length — every paragraph does something. The product blurb says nothing unnecessary. The headline variations show that choices are intentional, not arbitrary. The ability to write three different versions and explain the strategic difference between them is more useful in a communications role than any single polished piece.

Case Study · Research & Creative Lead · UBC Faculty of Arts · 2025
Linguistic Justice Awareness Project
Making accent discrimination research visible, usable, and actionable.

The brief: UBC researchers had conducted eight focus groups documenting how accent-based discrimination affected students' sense of belonging and academic participation. The data existed. The communications infrastructure to make it useful to instructors, administrators, and the public did not.

My role: Research and Creative Lead. I was responsible for brand voice, audience segmentation, content strategy across all channels, documentary production, and institutional distribution. I worked directly with the Project Director and collaborated with a small team of student researchers and a web developer.

The challenge: The audience was not one audience. Instructors needed practical tools. Administrators needed framing that connected to institutional equity commitments. Students needed to see their experience reflected without feeling tokenized. The public needed context without jargon. Each channel required different calibration of the same research findings.

What I built:

Public website
Thematic synthesis of research across five findings, structured for non-academic readers.
Instructor guide
Downloadable PDF with discussion prompts, classroom frameworks, and citation guidance.
Documentary film
Short documentary featuring student voices, distributed through UBC channels and screened at a public event.
Research report
Thematic analysis report synthesizing eight focus groups, written for institutional and academic audiences.

Outcomes: The project was distributed institutionally across UBC faculties. The Instructor Guide was adopted by the UBC Centre for Teaching, Learning and Technology for use in TA training programs. The documentary was screened at a public event attended by faculty, students, and administrators. The website remains live as a public resource.

What this demonstrates: Multi-channel campaign planning and execution from brief to launch. Research translation across audiences with different needs. Stakeholder coordination across a distributed team. Document and asset management across concurrent deliverables. And writing that works at both the institutional register (the report) and the human register (the documentary narration and website copy).

Strategic note
This project is the closest parallel I have to the customer advocacy and campaign coordination work described in most communications roles. The research played the role that customer interviews play in a corporate context: raw material that needed to be shaped, attributed, approved, and distributed through the right channels to the right people. The operational complexity — managing five deliverables, multiple stakeholders, an approval workflow, and a public launch — is the same regardless of sector.

Sourcing notes: finding quotes in call recordings and CRM

Search terms used when reviewing call recordings: "we used to," "now we," "the difference," "before Vela," "what changed," "I didn't expect."

These phrases flag moments where customers are narrating a before/after without prompting — the strongest raw material for advocacy quotes. Once identified, the timestamp is logged and the account owner is contacted to confirm the customer would be willing to have the quote attributed.

Voice note: strategic rationale
Customer advocacy programs that rely only on formal quote requests miss the majority of usable material. The best quotes happen in unguarded moments during discovery calls or check-ins. Building a habit of reviewing call recordings with specific search terms — and knowing what linguistic patterns signal a strong quote — makes advocacy sourcing systematic rather than opportunistic. The tracker ensures nothing falls through the cracks once a quote is identified, and that usage stays within the scope the customer approved.

Briefing Note · Confidential · Not for distribution
April 14, 2026

To: Senior Leadership Team

From: Employee Communications

Re: Flexible Work Policy Rollout — May 1

Purpose of this note: Prepare you for the all-staff announcement going out April 16, and for the questions your direct reports will bring you before May 1.

What is changing and why: Effective May 1, teams may agree on hybrid schedules at the team level, within parameters set by regional leadership. The three-in-office default remains in place for teams without a modified arrangement. This change responds directly to feedback from the 2025 employee survey — 61% of respondents flagged schedule flexibility as a priority. Framing it as a response to survey feedback is important. It signals that the survey process produces real outcomes.

What is not changing: Existing schedules remain in effect until managers confirm any changes. No retroactive adjustments. No expectations change before May 1.

Three talking points for your team conversations:

1. "This came out of what you told us in last year's survey. We heard that flexibility matters to this team, and we've structured a change that gives you and your manager real agency over how that works."

2. "Your schedule doesn't change automatically. If you want to explore a modified arrangement, start that conversation with your manager before May 1."

3. "If you have questions I can't answer, Employee Communications has a FAQ you can share, and HR Business Partners are available for anything more specific."

Likely questions and suggested responses:

QuestionSuggested response
"Does this mean I can work from home permanently?"The three-day default stays. But if your team and manager agree on a different arrangement, you can request it through the new process.
"What counts as 'team-level' scheduling?"Your direct team — the people you meet with regularly. Not the broader department.
"What if my manager says no?"Regional leadership parameters apply. HR Business Partners can help clarify what's in scope.

What you need to do before April 16: Review the cascade brief linked in the Hub (under Workplace Policies → 2026 Updates) and confirm you're available to field team questions after the announcement goes out. If you'd like Employee Communications to attend your next team standup, reach out by April 15.

Questions? Contact Employee Communications at [email protected] or your HR Business Partner.

Voice note: strategic rationale
Executive briefing notes are a different format from all-staff announcements — they're shorter, more direct, and focused on preparing a messenger rather than informing an audience. The talking points in this note are written to be spoken, not read aloud verbatim, so they're conversational rather than formal. The Q&A table gives leaders something to reach for when they're caught off guard, which reduces the likelihood of inconsistent messaging across teams. The action step at the end is specific and time-bound: that's intentional, because vague asks don't get done.

Beginning May 1, updated guidelines for hybrid and remote work arrangements will apply across all Canadian Vela offices. These changes reflect feedback from the 2025 employee survey and are designed to give teams more clarity and consistency around how flexibility works at Vela.

The core change: Teams may now agree on hybrid schedules at the team level, within parameters set by regional leadership. The expectation of three in-office days per week remains the default: but teams with specific project or client needs can request a modified arrangement through their manager.

What stays the same: Your existing schedule continues unchanged until your manager confirms any adjustments. No action is required before May 1 unless your manager has already been in touch with you directly.

What to do if you have questions: Start with your manager. If you need further guidance, your HR Business Partner can help. A full FAQ covering common scenarios: including client-site expectations, part-time arrangements, and international travel: is available on the Hub under Workplace Policies.

For managers: A brief for cascading this update to your teams is available on the Hub alongside the policy document. If your team has concerns you are not sure how to address, HR Business Partners are available for one-on-one support before May 1.

We know policy updates can create questions we may not have anticipated. We will send a follow-up post on May 1 with any clarifications that have come in since this announcement.

Questions? Contact Employee Communications at comms@vela.com or your regional HR Business Partner.

Voice note: strategic rationale
The highlight block isolates the single most important piece of information so readers The manager section at the end is deliberate. Most policy announcements treat managers as passive recipients. Giving them a cascade brief and HR support contact turns them into active communicators — and reduces the volume of questions that reach the central team. Specifying the follow-up date is one of the most commonly skipped steps in policy communication, and a major driver of repeat inquiries.
